Arthur James Whitmore

Born 1941, Somerset, England

Arthur James Whitmore was born on a grey February morning in 1941 in the village of Cheddar, Somerset — a place famous for its caves, its gorge, and, he would tell you firmly, its cheese. He was the eldest of four children in a household that was short on money but long on books, laughter, and the particular chaos that comes with a dog, several cats, and a hen that had decided the kitchen was its domain.

He attended the local grammar school, where an English teacher named Mr. Pemberton — perpetually dusty, perpetually enthusiastic — introduced him to Dickens, Hardy, and the idea that ordinary lives were extraordinary material. It was a lesson that stayed with him.

After completing his national service, Arthur trained as a surveyor and spent the better part of his working life in that profession, raising a family in the process, travelling when he could, and paying very close attention to everything along the way. He had a gift for noticing the telling detail — the raised eyebrow, the hesitation before a lie, the way a place smells different on a Sunday morning.

He has been married to Margaret for over fifty years. They have three children and seven grandchildren, each of whom has at some point been told a story at bedtime that went on rather longer than planned. He remains unapologetic about this.

He began writing his stories down in earnest after retirement, encouraged by his family to stop keeping them to himself. What follows on this site is the result — a collection assembled over several years of quiet mornings, strong tea, and the particular pleasure of getting things right.
