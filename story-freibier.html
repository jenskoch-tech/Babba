School Years · 1960s

A Trip across the Border

Danish Farm Life

✦

One year we were looking again for another summer job. As it turned out my class mate’s father was a brew master of the Binding Brewery, one of the two local competing major breweries in Frankfurt am Main , the other one was the Henninger Brewery. Yes,we got a job at the Binding Brewery.

We started with rolling full 50l beer kegs to the loading deck and rolling back empty kegs to the brew station to be cleaned and properly refilled. After that we were placed at the conveyor belt among other female and male workers from various countries such as Yugoslavia, Greece, Turkey etc.

The huge conveyor belt usually transported refilled flip-top half liter bottles from the filling machine but it happened that a bunch of those bottles were not properly closed and therefore needed to be closed by hand by the crew lined up at the belt and then placed in the cases at the end of the belt. We two poor lads had of course at the beginning no idea how to do this.

First we tried to grab one bottle with the left hand and one with the right hand. That did not work as the belt moved faster than we could catch those stubborn bottles and we missed to close a lot of them. The other workers were laughing at us but then showed us how to do it. The trick was to crisscross your arms and then catch the first approaching not closed bottle with the right hand and simultaneously the other bottle with the left hand.

It was painful and we needed quiet some bandages to cover the blisters. After the first week we managed to handle this as it became a routine. The last shift on Friday had an early end and when the belt finally stopped we were so relieved. By then we were also accepted by the crew of the belt to sit down with them and have some free beers. At that point there was a lot of laughter while dirty jokes were told - to our surprise mainly by the female workers.

At the end of the shift I had to get home by street car. There I was usually standing at the open platform to catch some fresh air. Also to my rescue there was a pole to hold tightly on to. Otherwise there was a clear and present danger that I would have fallen out of the tram due to the previous consumption of a quantity of brewing products.

However, working at the brewery there was one additional benefit besides getting payed. All employees were entitled to take home every day six half-liter bottles of free beer as an incentive with the purpose to enhance the motivation to work. This provision was under the strict condition that you had to bring back six empty bottles the next day if you wanted to receive another six bottles. At the beginning of the supply of free beer to the members of my family everybody happily enjoyed he extra provision of the beverage at no monetary contribution. After the end of the second week I sensed some reluctance from some family members to accept and continue to consume this fine liquid. Suddenly not all of the six bottles were emptied. First the contents of one and then of two of the bottles had to be disposed into the sink. What a waste!

I hoped that would only be the case as a temporary resolution. To my disappointment the situation did not improve. Then I learned that I could exchange the empty bottles not for the fine brewed Pils or Lager beer and asked to be supplied with a non-alcoholic malt beverage instead.

The malt beverage is sweet while tasty and also good for children. The switch worked fine for another week but then even my sister’s little daughter showed some dislike versus the malty beverage demanding alternate drinks such as milk. What a spoiled kid!

At the end of our job at the brewery my family’s outcry caused by desperation turned out to be:
” Kein Freibier mehr” (“No more Free Beer”).
The rumor says that it took some time for members of my family – including myself - to overcome the animosity versus a specific beverage but at some point the healing point was progressively reached.
