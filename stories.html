Memoir · 1964

Cover Charge

Three young men with a map, a compass none of them could read properly, and far too much confidence — a recipe, as it turned out, for one of the more memorable evenings of Arthur's early life.

Years ago one Saturday night we did some bar hopping in downtown Sarasota, Florida and ended up in this Rock’n Roll joint where a local live band was playing hot music. Since dancing made me a little sweaty I tried to get some fresh air and walked towards the entrance. At that hour there were not too many people yet but suddenly the place filled up quickly. Then while I was standing at the entrance and watch more and more people desired to come in, I instinctively had the idea to do something for my additional enjoyment. I stretched my body upright while standing at the entrance so nobody could get passed me. When the next couples approached I simply asked them: “The cover charge is $5.00”. Since there was no sign demanding the charge they looked at each other in disbelieve and then reluctantly reached out for their wallets to hand-over the five dollar charge. I then started laughing and told them that I was kidding. They showed quiet a bit of relieve not to have to make that payment. I was satisfied too since it proved that my little trick it would have worked to my financial benefit. However, that reminded me of something similar from the past. In high school we again needed to overcome the usual shortage of pocket money and had to come up with a marvelous idea to improve our financial situation. At that time it was very popular to look for entertainment outside of the downtown Frankfurt’s establishments as they were not so much affordable. So the villages around in the vicinity of Frankfurt always had dancing events on the weekends which we gratefully attended. This was happening despite the occasional menacing attitude of the local guys who groundlessly feared the competition when it came to the their girls. Their male instinct sometimes generated aggression that forced us to flee the scene promptly to avoid bodily harm. This scenario made us thinking and we told ourselves: “Why do we have to go to these places?”, “Let people come to our place!” Consequently, we planned our own dance events. We started out by renting a gymnasium in one of the nearby villages, prepared everything to promote the event, hired a rock band to play live, announced the details of the event including the cover charge. That very Saturday we nervously were sitting at the entrance door of the gymnasium waiting for the crowd to come. Slowly but steady they came. Everybody got a stamp on their back hand as proof for payment of the cover charge. Our first event had about 300 people, the next even more. What a success we had, we were overwhelmed with joy and excited about our newly acquired wealth. It could not get any better or…? One day we were informed by GEMA (“Die deutsche “Gesellschaft für musikalische Aufführungs- und mechanische Vervielfältigungsrechte”)(What a title! )(Society for Musical Performing and Mechanical Reproduction Rights), an organization that protects the copy rights and service rights e.g. for musicians, if you play music during a public event. GEMA fees accruing in connection with any media use shall be borne by the responsible party organizing the event. The fees depend on various factors such as the size of the room used for the event, the amount of the entrance charge and so on. In case you do not report the event the GEMA will charge you double the original fee. This was an unpleasant surprise and when we were confronted with the charges we were shocked. We already had paid the live band for the first event 2,000 Deutschmark and on top of that had to deal with all the other expenses. We calculated back and forth but the result did not change. This enterprise was over and out. The fees and charges that we as self-made entrepreneurs were confronted with left almost no money from all the cover charges we had collected.

Childhood · 1948

The Hen Who Lived in Our Kitchen

Her name, insofar as any of the family agreed on it, was Edith. She had very definite opinions about the furniture and no respect whatsoever for mealtimes.

In the autumn of 1948, when I was seven years old, a Rhode Island Red hen of formidable personality entered our lives and did not leave for the better part of two years. Her name, insofar as there was consensus on the matter, was Edith — my sister called her Beatrice, my youngest brother called her Chicken, and my father, on the occasions when she stood on his newspaper, called her several other things entirely.

She had arrived as one of a pair my mother obtained from the farmer at the edge of the village, in one of those postwar schemes to keep a household running on ingenuity and the occasional egg. The other hen — whose name I no longer remember, if we ever gave her one — behaved normally, lived in the garden, and eventually provided a Sunday lunch of no particular memory. Edith had other ideas.

Within a fortnight she had established that the corner beside the range was hers, that she was entitled to whatever crumbs fell from the kitchen table, and that the sound of my mother peeling potatoes was an invitation to supervise at close quarters. She was not, my mother said, a pet. She was a farm animal who had made a series of unilateral decisions about her domestic arrangements.

She was, I should add, an excellent layer. Whatever eccentricities Edith had indoors, she was reliable in the one matter that counted. My father, who complained about her constantly and with considerable creativity, was also observed on more than one occasion sharing his toast with her while reading his paper on a Saturday morning.

She died peacefully in the spring of 1950, of what I believe was simply old age. There was a brief silence at dinner the first evening she was gone. My father remarked that it was "quite peaceful, actually," and then looked slightly guilty about it. My sister cried. I ate my supper and thought about her, and how odd it is the things you miss — not the grand events, but a small warm shape in the corner of a winter kitchen.
