Memoir · 1960s

Cover Charge

A small act of mischief at a Sarasota rock bar one Saturday night sends Peter's memory back to his school days in Frankfurt — when he and his friends decided that instead of scraping together pocket money to attend dances, they would simply throw their own. It worked brilliantly, right up until the moment GEMA arrived with a bill and a very long name.

Years ago one Saturday night we did some bar hopping in downtown Sarasota, Florida and ended up in this Rock’n Roll joint where a local live band was playing hot music. Since dancing made me a little sweaty I tried to get some fresh air and walked towards the entrance. At that hour there were not too many people yet but suddenly the place filled up quickly. Then while I was standing at the entrance and watch more and more people desired to come in, I instinctively had the idea to do something for my additional enjoyment. I stretched my body upright while standing at the entrance so nobody could get passed me. When the next couples approached I simply asked them: “The cover charge is $5.00”. Since there was no sign demanding the charge they looked at each other in disbelieve and then reluctantly reached out for their wallets to hand-over the five dollar charge. I then started laughing and told them that I was kidding. They showed quiet a bit of relieve not to have to make that payment. I was satisfied too since it proved that my little trick it would have worked to my financial benefit.

However, that reminded me of something similar from the past.

In high school we again needed to overcome the usual shortage of pocket money and had to come up with a marvelous idea to improve our financial situation.
At that time it was very popular to look for entertainment outside of the downtown Frankfurt’s establishments as they were not so much affordable. So the villages around in the vicinity of Frankfurt always had dancing events on the weekends which we gratefully attended. This was happening despite the occasional menacing attitude of the local guys who groundlessly feared the competition when it came to the their girls. Their male instinct sometimes generated aggression that forced us to flee the scene promptly to avoid bodily harm.
This scenario made us thinking and we told ourselves: “Why do we have to go to these places?”

“Let people come to our place!”

Consequently, we planned our own dance events. We started out by renting a gymnasium in one of the nearby villages, prepared everything to promote the event, hired a rock band to play live, announced the details of the event including the cover charge. That very Saturday we nervously were sitting at the entrance door of the gymnasium waiting for the crowd to come. Slowly but steady they came. Everybody got a stamp on their back hand as proof for payment of the cover charge. Our first event had about 300 people, the next even more. What a success we had, we were overwhelmed with joy and excited about our newly acquired wealth. It could not get any better or…?

One day we were informed by GEMA (“Die deutsche Gesellschaft für musikalische Aufführungs- und mechanische Vervielfältigungsrechte”) (What a title!) (Society for Musical Performing and Mechanical Reproduction Rights), an organization that protects the copy rights and service rights e.g. for musicians, if you play music during a public event.
GEMA fees accruing in connection with any media use shall be borne by the responsible party organizing the event. The fees depend on various factors such as the size of the room used for the event, the amount of the entrance charge and so on. In case you do not report the event the GEMA will charge you double the original fee.

This was an unpleasant surprise and when we were confronted with the charges we were shocked. We already had paid the live band for the first event 2,000 Deutschmark and on top of that had to deal with all the other expenses. We calculated back and forth but the result did not change. This enterprise was over and out. The fees and charges that we as self-made entrepreneurs were confronted with left almost no money from all the cover charges we had collected.

Memoir · School Days, Frankfurt

Report Cards

Shredding his old school report cards, Peter reflects on an education system that had students reciting "I am a donkey" in Latin, teachers hurling keys across the classroom, and very little room for disagreement.

Recently I came across my old school report cards. After having glanced through all of them I decided to shred them all. I felt that there was no purpose to keep them any longer. But that destructive act initiated some reminiscent reflections while I came also to some conclusions.

Schools are non-democratic institutions with an autocratic principal who rules like an absolute reign. He directs the teachers, penalizes the pupils where deemed appropriate and lectures parents as they - in his judgement - lack the ability to provide their child with the necessary physical and mental tools to fit into the educational process. The scholastic institution is not designed to grant any tolerant rights other than claiming to provide the opportunity to gain profound wisdom which is also named and framed education. There is no room for Liberté, égalité, fraternité — liberty, equality, fraternity! The pupils are not privileged to dispute the ratings given by the teachers nor are they entitled to have the legal assistance of an attorney for that purpose. Even any medical statements from doctors as an explaining excuse for the pupil's eventual lesser achievement for any given task are not accepted or actually would be ignored. Any meeting with the principal or teacher and parents usually ends with no favorable positive result on behalf of the student. In the contrary, the interference of parents often creates some frustration on the principal's or teacher's side. As an effect the pressure from the authorities of the educational establishment on the pupil increases even more. Following this the youngsters have to develop stronger survival skills in confrontational situations to avoid any severe damage to their self esteem.

Here is an example how we were taught and treated by one of our teachers of the Latin language. That teacher was a heavy set man with extreme black hair who was originally from Romania. Every time he entered the class room an aura of fear accompanied him that lasted through the entire lesson. One of the first sentences he made the students repeat multiple times was: "Asinus est!", (In German "Ich bin ein Esel!"), (in English "I am a donkey!")

One can tell that this alone created a negative motion to overcome our dislike of a language no living human spoke in every day's life. Another Latin motto was: "Non scholae sed vitae discimus!" ("Nicht für die Schule, sondern für das Leben lernen wir!") ("We don't learn for the school but for life!"). Later when confronted with the realities of life it turned out to be not so true!

Another example of how difficult it was to cope with Mathematics. The math teacher scribbled endless formulas on the black board, suddenly turned around and pointed preferably at me first or my classmate who's last name followed me in the alphabetical order. While pointing at us the teacher's eyes behind his spectacles suddenly opened widely and almost seemed to pop out of the eye sockets. Then in a high pitched voice he shouted first my last name which is short and as he expected got no or rather the wrong answer which gave me a declassifying rating of "fueneff", meaning five. That on a scale from 1 to 6 is an insufficient answer. Then he turned to my classmate who's last name he stretched like Kraeeeeeeemerrrr! This guy shrugged in almost physical pain but also failed to give a correct answer which gave him what? "Fueneff" — meaning five.

These two examples stand for the mental abuse. But we also experienced physical abuse by our teacher for Geography who also was our teacher for "Leibeserziehung" ("Physical Education"). That's why he always wore his sweat suit even in Geography class. When he demanded answers to his geographic questions he called out one of us preferably who did not pay too much attention. Then he took a bunch of keys and threw them at the inattentive fellow whose attention improved instantly but his answer was anyway wrong.

However, we survived this experience and could face the threatening upcoming challenges of adult life a bit better following the saying: "What doesn't kill you makes you tougher!"

Memoir · Frankfurt, 1959

A Glorious Victory

Growing up an outsider in Frankfurt, Peter finds common ground with the locals through one shared religion — Eintracht Frankfurt. Then in 1959 the unthinkable happens, and the city's ancient fountain runs with beer and wine.

In February 1952 we moved from a little village in the Harz mountains to Frankfurt am Main. I was not even six years old but as I grew up there in Frankfurt am Main I consider it my hometown, even though I was not borne there. Usually if you are not a native Frankfurter, who some Non-Frankfurter call “Frankfurter Wuerstchen” (Frankfurter Sausage), you are not an “Eingeplackter”, you will always be a stranger and therefore will only reluctantly be accepted by the natives.

It did not help much to overcome their resentment if you tried to speak the local dialect. Even over time with improvement of your language skills the locals could still tell that you are not a “real” Frankfurter. However, we had in common our favorite soccer team the prestigious soccer club of Eintracht Frankfurt. At that time, at the end of the Fifties, the Bundesliga was not existent yet and “Die Eintracht” played in the highest German soccer League.

Every weekend we followed the game and even sometimes went to the stadium in Frankfurt when they played at home. The results were not always as expected and the fans had to deal with a loss and consequently ended up in the various pubs to put up with their disappointment by consuming lots of local beer or apple wine. Their complaint was always expressed by the gossiping remark: “Die Schlappekicker habbe widder verlore” (The lame kicker lost again). In case of a victory it turned out to be almost the same pattern while the mood was more joyful. So we accompanied the sportive events of our club every weekend no matter how.

Then one day in 1959 we witnessed a wonderful miracle. Our team, Eintracht Frankfurt, - to anyone’s surprise - won the German soccer championship.

We hardly could believe it! That needed to be celebrated. The victory celebration took place on the old Frankfurter Roemerberg with its beautiful old buildings and the fountain as the centerpiece. In 1959 Frankfurt had a population of 653,030 inhabitants and it looked like that on that very day of the celebration most of them attended this joyful event.

Following the opening remarks and presentation of the players holding high the well deserved trophy then the highlight of the day came as a wonderful surprise. It was announced that now the centerpiece fountain would be opened to quench the thirst of everybody for free.

But believe it or not — instead of water the fountain poured endlessly beer and wine. What a victorious party that was and nobody has ever afterwards seen so many happy Frankfurters.

First Jobs · 1960s

Tomatoes, Tomatoes, Tomatoes

Fresh from confirmation and legally old enough to work, Peter and his friend land their first job at a nursery — expecting to dig heroic holes for trees. Ten days later, they know one word very well indeed.

My friend and I just had passed the confirmation by the church and as one of the resulting privileges we now were legally allowed to work. But how to find a job?

One day we saw a big black luxury BMW limousine passing by our apartment block and in the back seat was a middle aged gentleman. Asking the neighbor who is that she replied: This is Mr. Platzdasch he owns the nursery down where you guys always hanging around doing whatever you doing. Mmh…?

That gave us the idea to go there and ask for a job as we needed a little more money as the pocket money was not enough to cover our expenses for certain goods. Even though we did not have the chance to talk to the owner we got accepted to work there for 10 days during our school vacation for I believe as for 1 Deutsch mark per hour.

We were highly motivated to join the labor force and prepared to dig big holes for the purpose of planting trees and huge bushes and we had the physical strength to do so.

The first day of our job turned out to be slightly different than expected. Upon our arrival at the nursery we were shown to a large wooden table. On the table’s one side was a huge pile of dark almost black soil, next to it were numerous little planting pots and on the other end scattered out some kind of green little plants.

We were then instructed about our task. What did we have to do?

First you take one of the little planting pots, then fill the pot with the beautiful dark soil. After that you aim with your pointing finger at the center of the pot filled with soil and push a hole into the soil but not too deep and not too low. To finalize the process you stick the green tiny plant into the hole and shuffle a bit of soil on top to stabilize the fragile plant so it could grow.

After 10 days of monotonous labor we surely knew the name of the little green plant:
Tomatoes, Tomatoes, Tomatoes…

School Memoirs · 1960s

Last Station — Suedbahnhof

The last years of high school, Peter and his friends discover a hidden bar behind a frosted-glass door at the railway station — 160 metres from school, warm, and serving beer at 7am. Mathematics never stood a chance.

The last years at High School the Saturdays were usually spoiled due to the fact that there were still lessons to attend and we usually needed to recover from Friday night. During the week we hardly could avoid to go to school but we progressively worked on that. From Monday to Friday our meeting point was the railway station at the Sued-Bahnhof (Railway Station South Frankfurt) which was also my last stop with the tram.

As school started at 07:30 am we had about 30 minutes to get properly prepared for the educational challenges of the day. Sometimes we had even more time for this purpose as we did not have the first lesson but just had to start with the second one.

When you entered the station especially during autumn and winter it was very dark and sparsely illuminated and you could hardly see the ticket booths on the right. But on the left we soon enough found out that there was a large two bi-folded wooden door with nontransparent glass panels which were presumably never touched by a caring hand. The first time we noticed that door we were naturally curious what we possibly would find behind those wing doors. By opening the door we - meaning usually 3 to 5 of the usual suspects - to our absolute surprise found out that we had come to a place which seemed to comfort us immediately.

The establishment turned out to be a Bar & Restaurant (“Bahnhofsgaststaette”) with five or six tables. What a hidden gem! At two of the tables some men were sitting there drinking beer and playing cards. They looked like bums but harmless. We slowly accommodated ourselves at one of the tables and since we felt kind of thirsty, following the example of the people at the other tables to blend in, we ordered a refreshing hops beverage.

After the first round of beers we suddenly realized that we had already missed the first class of the day. Then one of us could not hold back to mention that the first class of the day was actually a double lesson for the same subject such as mathematics. Who needs mathematics? So we decided to skip the second lesson as well.

In the meantime we had already decided to play cards like it was the case at the neighboring tables. We were totally convinced that we would learn from that more than from algebra. Playing cards you had to remember the cards played and watch the players not to cheat what occasionally happened. That required a lot of attention.

We finally even got accepted to the card games of the “professional” card players. The distance between Suedbahnhof and Freiherr-vom-Stein Gymnasium with 157.65 m (517.22 feet) seemed suddenly kind of far.

But when we finally made it to school we were in a very good mood and could easily tolerate the given lessons. Since we made these morning gatherings a habit we missed progressively more and more classes. That came to the attention of the teachers and they demanded an explanation.

That left us with no choice but to submit letters of excuse accordingly. However, that created some suspicion because the parents’ signatures had a deficiency of adult touch. Later after intensive questioning by our parents we confessed the evil deeds. But we got away with it because the parents covered it up as we would have gotten in even more trouble with the school authorities.

Frankfurt · 1960s

The Call

Talked into an Easter holiday job at Frankfurt's most upscale grocery store, Peter and his classmate spend two weeks at a calculating machine checking sales figures. The work is dull, the supervisor formidable — but one item on the daily ledger makes it all worthwhile.

One of my class mates father’s company was a supplier of a well known brand of quality sugar to Latscha, Frankfurt’s most upscale grocery stores. The father had good contacts to the management there. As the Easter Holiday was approaching the father - in agreement with my parents - felt that we should have another job for about the two weeks of the holidays.

His absolute goal was that we - instead of hanging around with laziness and pursuing useless non-profitable endeavors - be occupied in a more decent way. He also strongly believed that we should experience the realities of being part of the work force.

We reluctantly entertained the idea of getting another job but mainly due to the fact that our available pocket money was again on an extremely low level, but we agreed.

On the first day of work at Latscha’s accounting office the supervisor was a middle aged lady who welcomed us with a serious facial expression while speaking in a high pitched nasal twang voice which also had a slightly arrogant tone. Madam Supervisor then introduced us to her assistant accountant, “Frau Pfitzner”, who in turn instructed us what and how to do the job.

We each were seated on a small desk equipped with a calculating machine, a pencil, a pencil sharpener and an eraser. (No, there were no computers yet!).

All sales figures of all the product items sold in the Latscha stores on any given day had to be checked for accuracy and summarized. Therefore we had to enter all the sales figures into the calculating device: cling, cling, cling, cling and then bang — summed it all up and checked if correct. The figures were then forwarded to Frau Pfitzner who presented them to Madam Supervisor. The most fanciest item sold at Latscha’s was the La Croix Turtle Soup followed by a few other less popular items.

But when our favorite item had its turn Madam Supervisor shouted at her assistant: “Frau Pfitzner, die Putenoberschenkel!” (“Frau Pfitzner, the Turkey thighs!”). Frau Pfitzner every time shrugged sensitively and was obviously annoyed every time when this item was called. We on the other hand could hardly hold ourselves back from laughing but managed to hide our grin. Only after work when we relaxed with a drink or two we imitated Madam Supervisor’s call multiple times: “Frau Pfitzner, die Putenoberschenkel!” and then we really exploded with a hearty laughter.

Even until today I still have that call in my ear: “Frau Pfitzner, die Putenoberschenkel!” — and again have to laugh in fond memory.

Frankfurt · 1960s

The Peace Flag

In wartime, a white flag means surrender. For Peter's sister — newly moved into an apartment directly on his tram route home — it means something rather more demanding: the baby needs attention, and her teenage brother needs to jump off the tram immediately.

During previous war times it occasionally happened that one of the two adversaries needed to negotiate with the other party. Often the main purpose was to find out under what terms and conditions the battle and eventually the war could be ended. That was meant to be without deterioration of the loosing party’s combatant image.

How was that orchestrated? The one party interested in the formula for surrender pulled out a pole with a white flag attached to it. The presumably loosing party formed a delegation of a minimum of three soldiers, one officer, a sergeant and in addition an enlistee carrying the white peace flag. Under the given circumstances the negotiating parties respected the white peace flag and the members of the delegation were protected and should not be harmed whatsoever.

After the peace talk the delegation returned unharmed to their part of the battle field. Depending on the outcome of the dialogue with the hostile foe the fighting either continued or it ended as eventually agreed — thanks to the white flag as the symbol of peace.

I, however, personally suffered from a different experience with the white flag. How could that be?

As my beloved sister just had decided to move into a new apartment with her husband and her little daughter it turned out to be that the chosen accommodation was fortunately or rather unfortunately directly located on my way from school to home. The streetcar I had to take made two stops on that very road where the apartment block was located.

Occasionally I got off the tram to visit my sibling with her child, which made me her uncle. One day my sister asked me if I would help her once in a while in case she would face problems achieving her motherly duties for her child that was excessively demanding attention and nutrition. As an uncle of course I could not say no! But how would we arrange the rescue mission? Then she came up with this brilliant idea. She said to me: “Every time you pass by in your tram you should look at my front window and when you see a white flag then you know that is an emergency and I need your help immediately.”

I was almost overwhelmed with the sisterly trust. Since then every time I passed by I stared at my sister’s window with the expectation to see a huge white bed sheet hanging out of the window as a desperate outcry for rescue. But whenever I saw that white monster fabric flattering in the wind I was slightly in panic and murmured to myself: “Da hat die ja schon wieder die Friedensfahne rausgehaengt!” (“Now she hung up the peace flag again!”).

Then I jumped in hectic out of the tram - sometimes I missed the first stop and had to get off at the next - and rushed hastily to the building and up the stairs to provide my brotherly aid.

But believe me, after a while when I did not see the emergency signal I was kind of relieved and even reduced the occasional voluntary visits to my sister to a minimum.

Fortunately, some time later my sister, in dislike of the living location, moved closer to our parents where our mother as the grandmother was blessed to take over the privilege to serve as emergency help when needed.

However, one can tell that the white peace flag works not only during the battles of war but also during the daily challenges of peacetime.

Memoirs · 1970s

A Souvenir Photo

A summer holiday on the Costa Brava in the 1970s, where half of Germany has come to get a tan and prove it to the neighbours. Peter watches a fellow tourist named Werner attempt a simple beach photograph of his wife Erna — with consequences neither of them planned for.

We were so excited about our upcoming trip to Spain. The destination was Rosas/Costa Brava a vacation spot just across the Pyrenees mountains coming from France. The town of Rosas was also known as “Saarbruecken 3”. Why? From Saarbruecken as the most Western city of the Saarland it was roughly 1000 kilometers and at that time in the 1970s it could be driven in about 12 to 14 hours.

Rosas has a nice scenery with the Pyrenees mountains in the background and the beach of the Mediterranean Sea. The vacationers came from the United Kingdom, the Netherlands but mainly from Germany. Some of them took excessive advantage of the inexpensive – not to say cheap – liquor. Just like the British young lady expressed it perfectly when she finally came out of her hotel room around noon: “I am drunk, tipsy and tired.”

But the majority of all vacationers had one common goal: To get as much sun tan as possible. So you spend most of the day laying on your towel tossing and turning, sun creaming and tanning even with the risk of a severe sunburn. Occasionally you jumped into the water with the purpose of cooling down the already overheated body. But then immediately continued the process of intense tanning. Upon return to your homeland regardless of any skin damage you had to show off that you have had the privilege to spend a vacation in the sun in the South. Members of the family, friends and neighbors needed to be jealous otherwise you did not feel any satisfaction.

Another instrument to prove that one was vacationing in the South was to take souvenir photos. There was this German couple, Werner and Erna, standing at the beach while Werner intended to take a photo of his beloved Erna. He had one of these – at the time very common – one-way cameras. However, Werner directed Erna who was already standing in the water facing the beach and her back to the water to move further into the water. Already impatient as he was still not satisfied with her position he shouted at her: “Erna, geh mal ein Stueck zurueck!” (“Erna, move a little further back!”). Erna reluctantly moved backwards a few tiny steps expecting Werner to finally shoot the souvenir photo for everlasting memory.

But Werner again was not pleased with her position and demanded now in an even stronger tone while waving his arms in a pushing manner: “Erna, geh doch nochmal ein Stueck zurueck!” (“Erna, move again a little further back!”). Erna cooperated and followed Werner’s instruction by stepping backwards another few steps. It was one step too far. Suddenly Erna had disappeared into the obviously deeper water. Werner in fear his beloved Erna would drown dropped the precious camera, ran into the water and dived courageously into the spot where Erna had gone under. He pulled Erna out of the watery element and saved her but the spectators on the beach had a good laugh.

We will never know if Werner ever took another souvenir photo during their Spanish vacation.

Reflections

O.I.T.

Watching a Western one afternoon, Peter picks up a piece of wisdom from a screen Indian that has served him well ever since — in meetings, in tight spots, and anywhere a confident-sounding answer is required but none is available.

As a fan of Western movies I watched one of this genre the other day. There was a scene towards the dramatic end of the film that I liked very much. The Indian and the white man hiding in the bushes and observing the camp where the bad guys held the beautiful girl hostage. The bandits were heavily armed and outnumbered our two heroes.

Watching the outfit they realized that their chances were slim to overrun those kidnappers. The white man asked the Indian: “What we gonna do?” The Indian replied: “Don’t know!”

But then he said: “Maybe we should do an OIT!?” The white man frowning: “What in the hell is an OIT?” “You don’t know what an OIT is?” was the Indian’s answer in return. “No, I don’t!” was the response.

The Indian: “An OIT is an Old Indian Trick!” That sounded mysterious but also promising and it possibly could help to improve their chances to free the captured beauty.

Now the cowboy needed an explanation as to what the OIT actually was and waited for the Indian to release more details of the secret problem solution. The native reluctantly but with a sudden smile on his face gave it away: “I honestly don’t know but we always use this phrase when we absolutely don’t know what to do!”

Even though that was not much of a help it obviously encouraged the two brave men to fight the evil crowd and they finally managed to free the captured lady.

I took a useful lesson from this. So every time somebody asked me for help to solve a problem I always gave the following advice: “We probably need to do an OIT!”

The expected reaction consequently is: “What in the world is an OIT?” My reply spontaneously is: “OIT is an Old Indian Trick!” “What does it even mean?” Then I just repeat the Indian’s response: “I honestly don’t know but I always use this phrase when I absolutely have no idea what to tell someone what to do!”

So in case you are in a situation like that it might work if you apply this phrase. As a substantial benefit of the OIT you will not only get the attention of the ones you address but eventually also a good laugh. It is worth trying even in a serious business meeting when you are required to come up with a sophisticated solution. You might be pleasantly surprised about the reaction of your colleagues and superiors as you proclaim: “We probably need to do an OIT!” Just let me know how it worked!

The Egypt Years · 1988–1993

French Doors

Living in Cairo in 1991, Peter needs a French door fixed and is sent Ali the handyman — a man of great faith, no tools, and a remarkable talent for optimism. It takes one year, one earthquake, and no tools whatsoever to prove Ali right.

In 1991 we lived in Cairo in an apartment building with just five stories while next door was a higher building with a huge open garage beneath the building.

After the First Gulf War had ended, numerous Egyptian workers were forced to leave Iraq and Kuwait and to return home to Egypt. Most of them had no real place to stay and so some of them moved into the garage next door. After a while that became annoying due to the noise and smell from the “out-door cooking”. We finally decided to move out and move into a new apartment that was just completed with fresh paint and all.

There was just a small problem: One of the French doors leading to the balcony did not open or close properly. I mentioned that to the owner and landlord who promised to send Ali the handyman to fix it.

A few days later Ali showed up friendly smiling and devotedly approaching me. I showed him the door and he eagerly opened and closed the door a few times and then said: “Mish quwais” (Not good). I responded: “Ana arif” (I know this) and “That is why you’re here!”

Then he asked: “Indak tools?” (Do you have tools?) I said: “I have tools but where are yours?” His reply was: “Ma’a fiche tools!” (I have no tools). “Yalla, go get your tools.” Ali: “Hamza minutes, Insha’Allah” (Back in five minutes, God willing).

Due to my previous painful experience with Egyptian locals when it came to the prediction of time and directions my expectations were not very high as for the actual time of Ali’s possible return.

Approximately three hours later Ali returned. I asked him for the reason of delay and he pointed out that he had to go to the mosque and pray as required by the Muslim religion. Ali’s intense praying must have occupied his mind to a high extent as he forgot to bring the tools necessary for the repair.

In this moment I felt a strong emotion and forceful desire envisioning to strangle Ali to death with my bare hands. Fortunately my self-control mechanism worked in favor of Ali. I just breathed heavily and yelled at him: “Chalas” (That’s it, get out!)

One year later in October 1992 Cairo was struck by an earthquake of 5.8/5.9 and our apartment building was shook up quite a bit. Miraculously after that the French door opened and closed smoothly.

In the end Ali was probably right: “Who needs tools to fix a French Door?”

The Egypt Years · 1988–1993

Choice

When the German Minister of Defence visits Cairo, Peter's job is to make sure everything runs smoothly — the suite, the security, the dinner. It does. Then comes the after-dinner cognac order, and a Finance Office letter four weeks later that nobody quite expected.

During my time at the Embassy of the Federal Republic in Cairo from 1988 until 1992 there was - among others - one important event. The German Minister of Defense was due for an official visit to Egypt. This was a challenge especially for us as the military staff at the Embassy. On one hand we were excited because of the importance of the visit which on the other hand would also make us feel more important. But we knew the challenge. Consequently, we worked intensively to get all the details to ensure a successful visit.

Besides all the security measures we needed to find the right accommodation. When we found out that the General Manager of the Nile Hilton was a German National we picked that hotel. Our decision was based on the smart assumption that in case of any unfavorable circumstances we could deal easier with the eventual problems as we would not encounter any language problems.

In the morning on the day of the long awaited visit we went to the hotel to secure the last details to ensure a safe and pleasant stay for the Minister. At the hotel we met with the General Manager and tried to inspect mainly the suite that was reserved and supposedly prepared according to the agreement with the hotel. When we tried to enter the suite we could not. Shortly after we experienced that the room was not empty.

Finally we had access to the room and found an Arabian lady laying on the bed surrounded by multiple plates loaded with food and drinks. We were shocked then and even more when we heard that the Arabian Princess did not want to leave as she was supposed to that day by 11:00 am. The General Manager apologized but mentioned that he could not do much as that lady comes every year spending tons of money at the hotel and to force her to leave would be a disaster and a loss of future potential income.

We suddenly had reasonable doubts if we had made the right choice with this particular hotel. But the General Manager promised to find an alternate suite immediately which he fortunately managed to accomplish. Finally the new suite and all other rooms for the delegation including one room that I with two secretaries intended to use as an office were secured.

Actually, the first day of the visit went very well and in the evening after an official dinner the Minister and his delegation finally came back to the hotel. After a while the Minister’s Aide de Camp, a Lieutenant-Colonel, came to the office and asked me: “The Minister would like to have a cognac as a digestive after an opulent dinner, can you arrange that?”

I replied: “Sure, do you want single or double?” “Make it double!”

“Jawohl, do you want Egyptian or French cognac?” “French cognac would be fine!”

To make sure I seriously pointed out: “So, that would be 12 double French cognac, correct?” (I did not even smile). The answer was: “Yes.” The order was placed and delivered in a very short while and was obviously very much enjoyed. Everybody seemed to be happy with that choice.

However, about four weeks after a successful visit we at the Embassy received a letter from the Finance Office of the Ministry of Defense demanding strongly an explanation for the expenses of 1,200.00 Egyptian Pounds (at that time equivalent to 600 German Marks) for twelve double French cognac consumed by the Minister and his delegation during the stay at the Nile Hilton Hotel in Cairo.

At that point I could not help it but I had to laugh. Then my boss worried how we could possibly explain these high expenses. I replied: “That is easy, I specifically gave them the choice between Egyptian and French cognac and also the choice between single or double. That’s what they wanted and that’s what I ordered on their behalf. Nobody asked me how much it would cost!”

We replied to that letter in a slightly different formal way but the tenor was the same: “It was their choice!” We never ever heard back anything about this issue.

The Egypt Years · 1988–1993

Cultural Differences

A German engineer visits an Egyptian farmer and offers to replace his donkey with a modern water pump — a generous leap from ancient tradition into the twentieth century. A year later he returns to find the donkey back at the well, and the explanation is faultless.

There was this Egyptian Fellahin, a common farmer, who had a donkey to draw water from his well in order to provide the precious fluid to the fields to make the vegetables grow. The donkey while attached with a harness to a wooden post and the well had to walk around the well in circles permanently to draw the water that was desperately needed.

That was a tradition since ancient times and had worked very effectively over centuries.

But one day the Fellahin encountered a visitor from a Western country who turned out to be an engineer from Germany, a country as we all know with well educated and highly sophisticated people of all kinds of different professions.

This engineer watched the donkey a couple of days and then approached the Fellahin and gave him professional advice. He said, Ali — that was the Fellahin’s name — and pointed at the donkey: Ali, you have to get rid of the donkey because I will give you a motor to run in order to draw the water from the well faster and more effectively. That will give you and the donkey quite a relief. You just have to provide once in a while oil to the engine to make it run smoothly and effectively.

Ali was overwhelmed by this generous offer which transferred him into modern times. It also made him tremendously proud as he could now show off towards his neighbors who watched what was happening there with a lot of curiosity. The German engineer told Ali that he would be back in a year or so but advised him to follow his instructions on how to work and maintain the engine properly. Ali thankfully said farewell to the engineer and expressed his wish that the engineer would come back — Insha’Allah — one day.

Finally, after a year or so the German engineer came back to Egypt and was very keen to find out how Ali had managed the new device instead of the retired donkey. But to the engineer’s surprise when he came to the well there was the same old donkey drawing the water the same old fashioned way like centuries before. The German engineer was shocked and disappointed.

He asked Ali what had happened and why he did not continue to use the highly sophisticated modern pump. Ali responded honestly: Honorable Mohandessin (Engineer), I used this wonderful electric pump for quite some time but one day it just stopped. Why was that? The engineer asked — I told you how to do maintenance just by changing the oil once in a while! Then Ali responded regretfully: How can I do proper maintenance as you did not provide me the necessary oil, so I had to re-hire my old donkey!
