Travel · 1978

A Train Journey Through Portugal

On the slow train from Lisbon to Porto, and the kindness of strangers

✦

I was thirty-seven years old and travelling alone for the first time in several years — Margaret had stayed home with the children, a fact that made her, I suspect, considerably happier than she admitted — when I found myself at Santa Apolónia station in Lisbon at seven o'clock on a September morning, holding a ticket whose destination I could read perfectly well but whose train I could not locate anywhere on the board.

A railway employee, a small man with an enormous moustache and the air of someone who had long since made peace with the bewilderment of foreigners, pointed me toward a platform without looking up from his newspaper. The train was already there. It was older than I had expected, painted a dark green that might once have been cheerful, and it smelled pleasantly of wood and something I couldn't quite identify — coffee, possibly, or sunlit upholstery, or simply the accumulated warmth of many summer mornings.

I found a compartment with six seats, five of which were occupied. The sixth, by the window, was mine. My fellow passengers regarded me with the polite curiosity that the Portuguese seem to reserve for the English — a mixture of amusement and goodwill that I would encounter all week and never quite understand how to repay. A woman of perhaps sixty, dressed very formally for a Tuesday morning, offered me an orange from her bag before I had even sat down properly.

I accepted it with great enthusiasm and very poor Portuguese. She laughed. Her husband, sitting beside her and reading a newspaper folded to an improbable size, nodded at me solemnly over the top of it, as though confirming that yes, the orange was a good idea, and that I had made the right decision.

The train moved north through a landscape I was not prepared for. I had expected something drier, more Mediterranean. What I found instead was a country of surprising green hills and deep river valleys, of long stone walls and wild-looking countryside that reminded me, oddly, of parts of Wales. We stopped at stations whose names I tried to read before the train pulled away again, and at each one someone got off and someone got on, and the compartment's population shifted and reshuffled like a slow-moving card game.

At some point past noon — I had lost precise track of time, which seemed appropriate given the rhythm of the journey — we stopped at a small station for what turned out to be forty-five minutes. The reason for this was never announced, or perhaps it was announced in Portuguese and I understood nothing. My remaining fellow passengers did not seem concerned. The woman with the oranges produced a parcel of food from a large bag, unwrapped it with the confidence of someone who had done this on many such journeys, and handed me a piece of bread and a slice of cold meat without any discussion whatsoever.

We ate together in the warm compartment with the windows open, and she told me something at some length — gesturing with great expressiveness at the landscape outside and then at me — which I believe was either the history of the region through which we were passing, or a detailed account of her daughter's wedding, or both. I nodded at intervals. She seemed satisfied.

I arrived in Porto at half past three, considerably later than the timetable had suggested. I had eaten an orange, shared a meal with strangers, slept briefly against the window, and failed to understand a single word of at least four conversations. I had also, I realised as I walked out into the golden afternoon light of Porto's streets, had one of the finest days of that particular trip.

I have thought about that train journey many times since. What strikes me now, as it struck me then, is how little was required — a window seat, an orange, a landscape moving past, and the simple decency of people who had no particular reason to be kind to a stranger.

It was enough. It was more than enough.
